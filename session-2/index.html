What LLMs Are NOT Good At

Always verify these — never trust blindly

TaskWhy it's risky
Stating facts, statistics, datesMay confabulate plausible-sounding but wrong data
Recent eventsTraining data has a cutoff — it may not know
Calculations & mathsCan make arithmetic errors, especially multi-step
Legal / regulatory specificsRules change; model may be outdated or wrong
Citing sourcesWill sometimes invent citations that don't exist
Predicting outcomesNo real-world knowledge beyond training data

Key rule for BAs: If the output contains a number, a name, a date, or a specific claim — verify it independently before using it.

This isn't about distrust — it's about using the right tool for the right job. You wouldn't use Excel to write a narrative report; you wouldn't use an LLM as a source of truth for regulatory compliance. Clear boundaries make you more effective, not less.

What Are Hallucinations?

The model confidently says something that isn't true

LLMs generate the most statistically likely next token — they don't "look things up." When they don't know something, they don't say "I don't know" — they generate a plausible-sounding answer.

Analogy: Like a colleague who's brilliant at structuring arguments but will fill gaps in their knowledge with educated guesses — and won't always tell you when they're guessing.

Classic Examples
  • Invented statistics: "Studies show 73% of BAs use AI tools" — made up
  • Fake citations: "According to Smith et al. (2021)..." — paper doesn't exist
  • Wrong dates or names: Confidently incorrect factual claims
  • Outdated information presented as current

↓ Why this is a structural property, not a bug

The hallucination problem is not a bug that will be "fixed" — it's a fundamental property of how LLMs work. The solution is workflow design, not model improvement. That's what the next slide covers.

Why Hallucinations Happen

Understanding the root cause helps you prevent it

How LLMs generate text

LLMs predict the most statistically likely next word based on patterns in training data. They are not searching a database — they are completing a pattern. They cannot distinguish "I know this" from "this sounds right."

Where it goes wrong

When asked about something rare, recent, or specific, the model still generates a confident-sounding pattern — but the underlying "fact" may not exist. It fills gaps with plausible-sounding text.

Key insight: Hallucination is not random — it's a structural property. The model cannot flag its own uncertainty reliably. The solution is in how you design your prompts.

Once participants understand hallucination as a structural property, not a random flaw, they'll naturally move towards the mitigation strategies on the next slide.

How to Prevent Hallucinations

4 practical techniques for reliable output

1 Ground the model with your own data

Paste the actual document, data, or facts into the prompt. Model can only draw on what you give it.

"Based only on the following document, answer this question: ..."

2 Ask it to flag uncertainty

Explicitly instruct it to say "I don't know" when uncertain.

"If you are not certain, say so. Do not invent statistics."

3 Break complex tasks into steps

Instead of one big prompt, build up reasoning incrementally.

Step 1: list themes → Step 2: find evidence → Step 3: summarize

4 Verify anything that matters

Any number, name, date, or specific claim used in a deliverable: check it.

Treat AI output like a first draft from a junior colleague
Technique 1 (grounding) is the most powerful. When you paste in the actual source material, the model is essentially doing comprehension and synthesis — not recall. That's where it's most reliable.

Deterministic vs Non-Deterministic Tasks

Match the task type to your verification level

Deterministic Tasks

Reformatting, extraction, translation, summarizing from given text. The "right answer" is obvious — you can check it at a glance.

→ Low verification burden

Non-Deterministic Tasks

Factual claims, analysis conclusions, recommendations, predictions. The "right answer" isn't obvious — it requires external validation.

→ Higher verification burden
"Is this task about shaping content I already have, or generating knowledge I don't have?"

Shaping

Use confidently, light verification. You own the source material.

Generating

Use as a starting point, verify before trusting. Model is recalling.

This is the most important mental model in the session. It doesn't require deep technical knowledge — just the habit of asking "where did this come from?" before using AI output in a deliverable.

Advanced Prompting: Role-Based Prompts

Give the model a perspective to work from

Basic instruction
"Review this requirements document."

Generic, helpful, polite response. No specific focus or critical angle.

Role-based
"You are a skeptical senior project manager who has seen many projects fail due to unclear requirements. Review the following requirements document. Flag any vague statements, missing acceptance criteria, and assumptions that could become problems during delivery."

↓ Why it works + multiple roles

Role-based prompting is particularly powerful for getting critical, constructive feedback — something LLMs naturally avoid unless you explicitly ask for it. BAs often need someone to challenge their work; this gives you that.

Why Role-Based Prompts Work

The role activates a specific frame of knowledge and attitude

What the role does

  • • Shifts the model's "perspective" and vocabulary
  • • Activates relevant domain knowledge and patterns
  • • Changes what the model prioritises in the output

Power technique: multiple roles

Review once as a BA, once as a developer, once as a QA tester — in a single prompt:

"Review this first as a BA, then as a developer, then as a QA tester. For each role, list the top 2 concerns."

"Skeptical senior PM" produces very different output from "helpful assistant" — same input, same model. The role is the key variable.

BAs often need critical feedback on their own work. Role-based prompting gives you a constructive critic on demand. The multiple-roles technique is particularly powerful for requirements review.

Advanced Prompting: Constraint-Based Prompts

Tell it what NOT to do

ConstraintWhat it prevents
"Do not add information not in the source"Hallucination
"Do not use jargon"Inaccessible output
"Flag assumptions explicitly, do not state them as facts"Hidden assumptions
"Do not exceed 200 words"Over-verbose output
"Ask me if anything is unclear before answering"Misunderstood tasks
"Do not recommend solutions — only identify problems"Premature conclusions

LLMs default to being helpful, comprehensive, and agreeable — constraints give you direct control over those defaults.

The last two are especially useful in BA work. "Ask me if anything is unclear" prevents the model from making assumptions about your intent. "Identify problems only" is great for gap analysis work.

Advanced Prompting: Output Format Control

Tell it exactly what structure you need

Tables

"Return the results as a table with columns: Requirement ID, Description, Priority, Owner, Open Questions."

Numbered Lists + Sub-Points

"Return as a numbered list. Under each point, add a sub-bullet with the business rationale."

Structured Document

"Use this exact structure: ## Executive Summary / ## Key Findings (bullet list) / ## Risks / ## Recommended Next Steps"

JSON / Structured Data

"Return as JSON with keys: title, summary, action_items, owner, deadline."

Once you know you always need output in a specific format — build that template into your prompt and reuse it.

Output format control is underused. Once you know you always need requirements in a specific template, build that template into your prompt and reuse it. This is the start of building personal "prompt templates" — which we'll build on in Session 3 with agents.

Building Your Own Prompt Templates

Stop rewriting prompts — build a library

Save your best prompts as reusable templates with placeholders. Copy-paste into any LLM tool as needed.

Where to store them
  • • A Word or Notion doc with prompts by use case
  • • Organised by task type (requirements, comms, analysis)
  • • Start building today — add to it after each session

This is the low-tech version of what an AI Agent does — a pre-configured prompt that runs consistently.

Example — Meeting Notes to Action Items
You are an experienced professional. Read the following meeting notes and extract: 1. A list of decisions made (with owner if mentioned) 2. A list of action items (format: Action | Owner | Due Date) 3. A list of open questions that need follow-up Do not add anything not in the notes. If owner or due date is not mentioned, write "TBC". Meeting notes: [PASTE NOTES HERE]
Encourage BAs to start building a personal prompt library today. By Session 3, they'll see how this scales into automated agents.

Using AI for Data in Excel

AI as your spreadsheet co-pilot

What AI can do with Excel

  • Explain what a formula does in plain English
  • Write a formula from a plain-English description
  • Suggest a chart type for a given dataset
  • Summarize trends in a table
  • Identify anomalies or outliers
  • Draft the narrative for a data section
Example Prompts
"Explain what this formula does: =SUMIFS(D2:D100, B2:B100, "North", C2:C100, "Q1")"
"I have a table: Date, Region, Sales Rep, Revenue. Write an Excel formula to calculate total revenue for North region in Q1 2025."
"Look at this sales data [paste table]. What are the 3 most notable trends you see?"
The key mindset shift: you don't need to know the formula syntax — you describe what you want and let the model write it. Verify the formula works, but you don't need to understand every character.

Using AI for Presentations

From brief to slide structure in seconds

What AI can help with

  • Generating a slide outline from a text brief
  • Writing concise slide titles and bullet points
  • Suggesting a logical narrative flow
  • Drafting speaker notes
  • Condensing dense documents into presentation content

What AI cannot do

  • Apply your company's slide template
  • Insert actual charts (can describe what to use)
  • Replace your judgment on what the audience needs
Example Prompt
You are an experienced professional preparing a presentation for leadership. Based on the following project summary, create an 8-slide outline. For each slide: - Slide title - 3-4 bullet points - One speaker note Tone: executive-level. Focus on decisions and outcomes, not process detail. [Paste project summary]
AI does the 80% heavy lifting — you do the 20% polish.
The fastest workflow is: use AI to generate the structure and content → paste into your template → refine. The AI does the 80% heavy lifting; you do the 20% polish. This is a 3x speed-up for most BAs.

Demo 1: Hallucination — Live Example

Purpose Show a hallucination happening — then show how grounding prevents it. Same model, same topic, completely different reliability.
Step 1 — Without grounding
Prompt
What were the key findings of the Gartner report on AI adoption in business analysis published in 2024?
AI Response (fabricated)
Step 2 — With grounding
Prompt
Based only on the following text, what are the key findings about AI adoption? Do not add any information not present in this text. If something is not mentioned, say "not covered in source." [Paste any real paragraph from a document you have]
AI Response (grounded)
Notice: Step 1 produced specific statistics, a named Gartner report, and confident findings — almost certainly fabricated. Step 2 stayed within the source. The change was entirely in the prompt structure, not the model.
What to highlight: The fabricated response in Step 1 looks convincing — specific percentages, report name, quarters. That's what makes hallucinations dangerous. Step 2 shows how one constraint ("based only on the following") eliminates the problem entirely. Same model, completely different reliability.

Demo 2: Role + Constraint Prompting

Scenario Review a draft requirements document critically — using role, constraints, and format control together.
Prompt
You are a senior developer who has been burned by vague requirements in the past. Review the following requirements document. Do not be polite — be direct. For each requirement, flag if it is: (a) clear and testable, (b) vague and needs clarification, or (c) missing acceptance criteria entirely. Return as a table: Requirement | Assessment | Specific Issue | Suggested Fix Do not add requirements that aren't there. Do not summarize — go requirement by requirement. Requirements: 1. The system should be fast 2. Users must be able to export reports 3. The dashboard should refresh every 5 minutes with live data from Salesforce 4. The UI should be user-friendly 5. Admins can manage user permissions
AI Response
Notice: Role ("burned developer") + constraint ("be direct", "do not summarize") + format ("table") combined into a genuinely useful review. Requirement 3 was correctly identified as the only clear, testable one.
What to highlight: How role + constraints + format control combines into a genuinely useful requirements review tool. The model correctly identified Req 3 as the only well-formed requirement. Run this live — let the output generate in real time.

Demo 3: Slide Outline from a Project Brief

Scenario Turn a raw project summary into an 8-slide executive briefing outline — in seconds.
Prompt
You are an experienced professional preparing an executive briefing. Based on the project summary below, create an 8-slide presentation outline. For each slide provide: - Slide title - 3 bullet points (max 10 words each) - One speaker note (1-2 sentences) Focus on business decisions and outcomes. The audience is senior leadership — no technical detail. Project summary: We are implementing a new customer data platform (CDP) to unify customer data across 4 systems: CRM, ERP, support ticketing, and web analytics. Aim: give sales and marketing a single customer view. Benefits: 20% reduction in manual data reconciliation, faster campaign targeting, improved retention. Timeline: 6 months. Budget: £400K. Key risks: data quality in legacy systems, team adoption.
AI Response
Ask the room: "What would you change?" — this is the moment where AI does the heavy lifting and you add the judgment. The structure is there; your expertise refines it.
What to highlight: How quickly a usable 8-slide structure appears from a single paragraph. Then ask "What would you change?" — shows that AI does the heavy lifting, the BA adds judgment. The model even inferred that Slide 7 should be the call-to-action.

Your Turn — Critical Review Exercise

5 minutes · Open any LLM tool you have access to

The Task

Paste a piece of your own work (a requirements section, a status update, any document) and use this prompt:

You are a critical but constructive reviewer. Review the following document. Identify: 1. Any statements that are vague or unmeasurable 2. Any missing information a developer or stakeholder would need 3. Any assumptions stated as facts Be specific. Return as a numbered list. Do not rewrite the document — only flag issues. [Paste your document here]
Debrief question: Did it catch anything you hadn't noticed?
Give them 5 minutes. Walk around and help. The most powerful moment is when the model flags something they thought was clear — that's the "aha" moment for this session. Common stumbling block: people aren't sure what document to use. Any work document works.
