What is AI?

AI is not one thing — it's a family of technologies

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Any system that mimics human decision-making

Machine Learning (ML)

AI that learns from data instead of following fixed rules

Deep Learning

ML using neural networks to find complex patterns

What is Generative AI?

GenAI is the layer that creates — not just classifies

Traditional AI

Given input → predicts a label or score

"Is this email spam?" → Yes / No
Generative AI

Given input → generates new content

"Write a professional reply to this email" → full draft

↓ What can GenAI generate?

The key shift is from classifying existing things to creating new things. This is why GenAI feels so different — it's not just answering yes/no, it's producing work.

How LLMs Work

An LLM is a very sophisticated autocomplete

1
Training
Reads billions of documents — books, websites, code, conversations
›
2
Pattern Learning
Learns which words, ideas, and structures follow each other
›
3
Generation
Predicts the most useful next tokens, one at a time

↓ The autocomplete analogy

Avoid math or diagrams of neural networks. The autocomplete analogy is enough. What matters is: it's not "thinking" the way humans do — it's sophisticated pattern matching at massive scale. This is important later when we talk about hallucinations.

The Autocomplete Analogy

Like a very well-read colleague who can complete your sentence —
but has read everything ever written, in every language.

What it IS doing

Sophisticated pattern matching at massive scale — predicting the most useful next word given everything it's seen

What it is NOT doing

"Thinking" the way humans do — it doesn't have beliefs, intentions, or real understanding

This is why LLMs can be confidently wrong (hallucinate) — they're completing patterns, not checking facts.

This distinction matters. Don't dwell on hallucinations — just plant the seed that the model is pattern matching, not reasoning. We'll revisit this in Session 2.

Tokens & Context Windows

Two concepts that explain LLM limits

Tokens

  • A token ≈ ¾ of a word
  • "Business Analyst" = ~3 tokens
  • Very large documents may be truncated

Context Window

  • The model's "working memory" per session
  • Includes your messages and its replies
  • Modern models: 100K–200K tokens (300–500 pages)
  • Exceed it → model forgets earlier messages
Practical tip: If the model seems to "forget" what you said earlier in a long session, start a fresh conversation and re-provide the key context.
Don't go deep here. Just make sure they understand why pasting a very large document might not always work as expected, and why context matters in long conversations.

Prompt Engineering

Garbage in, garbage out — but it's easy to fix

Role Who should it act as?

"You are a senior business analyst reviewing a requirements document."

Instruction What do you want?

"Review the following requirements and identify any ambiguities or missing information."

Context What background does it need?

"This is for an ERP migration project. The audience is the development team."

Output Format How should it respond?

"Return a numbered list of issues. Keep each point under 2 sentences."
Most people only provide the instruction. Adding role + context + output format dramatically improves results — and this works the same way in every AI tool you'll ever use.

Element 1: Role

"Who should the model act as?"

Setting a role shifts the model's perspective, vocabulary, and level of detail.

Generic

"Summarize this document."

→ Generic summary, no particular audience or purpose

With Role

"You are a business analyst. Summarize this document."

→ Focuses on requirements, stakeholders, business impact

Keep this snappy. The role is the first lever that makes a big difference.

Element 2: Instruction

"What do you want it to do?"

Be specific. Vague instructions produce vague results.

Vague

"Help me with this document."

Specific

"Identify all functional requirements, list any ambiguities, and flag any dependencies between requirements."

The instruction is the core of the prompt. Everything else supports it.

Element 3: Context

"What background does it need?"

The model only knows what you tell it. Background context shapes every decision it makes.

  • Who is the audience? (technical? executive? external?)
  • What is the project or situation?
  • What are the constraints? (deadline, budget, policy)
  • What has already been decided?
Context is where BAs have a natural advantage — they're used to gathering and providing context for stakeholders. Same skill applies here.

Element 4: Output Format

"How should it respond?"

Specifying format removes back-and-forth and gives you copy-paste ready output.

Format options
  • Bullet list / numbered list
  • Table
  • Paragraph (max N words)
  • Specific document sections
  • JSON / structured data
Example

"Return a numbered list of issues. Keep each point under 2 sentences. End with a one-paragraph overall risk summary."

Output format is the most overlooked element. When specified well, the output is often directly usable without editing.

Prompt: Before vs After

The same question — two very different results

Weak Prompt
"Summarize this document."
  • No role or perspective
  • No audience specified
  • No focus area
  • No output format
Strong Prompt
"You are a business analyst. Summarize the following requirements document for a non-technical stakeholder. Focus on business impact, not technical details. Use 5 bullet points, each under 20 words."
  • Role (BA context)
  • Audience (non-technical stakeholder)
  • Focus (business impact)
  • Output format (5 bullets, word limit)
Run this live — paste the same document into both prompts and show the difference in output. This is one of the most impactful moments in the session. The point is not "use this exact template" — it's "every piece of context you add makes the output more useful."

System Role vs User Message

Two channels to talk to an LLM

System Message

Sets persistent behavior for the entire conversation — the "configure" panel

"You are a BA assistant. Always respond concisely. Never make assumptions — ask for clarification instead."
User Message

Your specific task or question per turn

"Summarize the attached requirements document for a non-technical audience."
When you use a pre-built AI assistant, someone has already written a system message — that's what shapes its behavior. In Session 3, we'll use this directly when designing agents.
This is a bridge to Session 3 on Agents. Don't go deep — just plant the concept that there are two layers: permanent instructions and per-message instructions. This concept is identical across all LLM tools.

Context Carryover

The LLM remembers everything in your current conversation

How it works

  • Every message + every reply is appended to the context
  • The model sees the full history each time it responds
  • This is why "make it shorter" or "change the tone" works

Best practices

  • Start a new chat for a completely different task
  • Re-state important context in very long conversations
  • The model does not remember previous sessions (unless memory is explicitly enabled)
This explains a common frustration — "why did it forget what I told it?" — and sets up better habits. Consistent across all tools.

Few-Shot Examples

Show, don't just tell

Zero-shot (no example)
"Write a project status update."
Few-shot (with example)
"Write a project status update in this format: Status: [Green/Amber/Red] This week: [2 sentences] Next week: [2 sentences] Blockers: [if any] Here's a good example: [paste one] Now write one for: [paste your project details]"
Why it works: The model calibrates exactly to your format, tone, and length — no back-and-forth needed. Ideal for templated BA outputs: status reports, BRDs, meeting summaries.
Particularly powerful for BAs who produce templated outputs — status reports, BRDs, meeting summaries. Spend 2 minutes showing this live. Works the same in any LLM tool.

Chain of Thought Prompting

Ask it to think before it answers

The problem

For complex tasks, LLMs sometimes jump to a conclusion that's wrong

The fix

Add "think step by step" or "reason through this before answering"

"You are reviewing a business requirements document. Before giving your assessment, list the key business objectives, list the stated assumptions, and identify any gaps between them. Then provide your overall assessment."
📄 Analysing complex documents
⚖️ Comparing options
🔍 Diagnosing process issues
The single most underused prompting technique. Dramatically improves quality on analytical tasks — exactly what BAs do. Works identically across all LLM tools.

Demo 1: Drafting a Requirements Document

Scenario You've just had a kickoff meeting for a new reporting project. You have rough bullet notes. Let's turn them into a structured requirements document.
Prompt
You are a senior business analyst. Based on the following meeting notes from a project kickoff, draft a structured requirements document with these sections: Business Objective, Scope, Key Stakeholders, Functional Requirements (numbered list), Assumptions, Open Questions. Meeting notes: - Client wants a monthly sales dashboard - Should show revenue by region, product category, and sales rep - Data comes from Salesforce and the ERP system - Finance team and Sales Directors are the main users - Must be ready before end of quarter - Mobile access was mentioned but not confirmed - Historical data: last 2 years minimum
AI Response
Notice: It surfaced "mobile access" as an Open Question without being told to — because the prompt said mobile access was "mentioned but not confirmed." That's the model applying BA thinking.
What to highlight: Structure, professional tone, how it surfaced "mobile access" as an open question — without being told to. The model picked up on the uncertainty in the notes and flagged it correctly.

Demo 2: Summarizing for a Non-Technical Audience

Scenario You need to brief a CFO on a technical migration project. They don't want technical detail — just business impact, cost, and timeline.
Prompt
You are a business analyst. Rewrite the following technical summary for a CFO who is not technical. Focus only on business impact, cost implications, and timeline. Remove all technical jargon. Use plain English. Maximum 150 words. Technical summary: The proposed solution involves migrating the existing on-premise SQL Server database to Azure SQL Managed Instance. The migration will require a lift-and-shift approach with schema validation, ETL pipeline reconfiguration, and a parallel-run period of 4 weeks to validate data integrity. Estimated infrastructure cost reduction is 35% annually post-migration. Total project effort is approximately 800 person-hours over 12 weeks.
AI Response
Notice: It preserved the key numbers (35%, 12 weeks, 800 hours) and reframed entirely for a business audience — no SQL, no ETL, no schema. Same facts, different language.
What to highlight: How it strips jargon, preserves the key numbers (35%, 12 weeks), and reframes entirely for the audience. The "Recommended next step" at the end was inferred from context — not explicitly asked for.

Demo 3: Rewriting a Stakeholder Update

Scenario A team member sent you a status update that's too negative in tone. Same facts — better framing.
Prompt
Rewrite the following project status update. Keep all the facts but make the tone constructive and solution-focused. Do not hide problems — frame them as challenges being actively managed. Keep it under 100 words. Original: "The project is behind schedule because the dev team didn't deliver the API on time. We've been waiting 3 weeks and still don't have a confirmed date. Testing hasn't started and we're at risk of missing the go-live date. The client is frustrated."
AI Response
Notice: Every fact is preserved — the delay, the missing date, the testing gap, the client concern. But the language shifts from blame to accountability and next steps.
What to highlight: Same facts, completely different tone. This is a real BA skill — translating raw updates into stakeholder-appropriate communication. The model even added a "Next action" line as a professional touch.
